  • Overview of calligraphy of the countries
  • For VietNam

Vietnamese calligraphy is heavily influenced by Chinese calligraphy. Originated in China and Arabia. Since then, it has become a traditional art until today.

For VietNam

Vietnamese calligraphy was imported from China, during the Northern colonial period, the Han people spread both culture and writing into our country, including calligraphy. Chinese calligraphy developed widely in our country until Quoc Ngu was born and was gradually replaced by Quoc Ngu calligraphy.

Although originating from Chinese calligraphy, Vietnamese calligraphy has its own very different points, creating a unique feature that Chinese calligraphy cannot have. Vietnamese calligraphy is writing Latin characters, not hieroglyphs like Chinese characters, so you can freely create your own writing according to your wishes and feelings. When writing Vietnamese calligraphy, you are not tied to any certain framework compared to Chinese characters.

Currently, the art of calligraphy has been widely popularized and developed all over the country. It is no longer limited by the idea that calligraphy is an ancient art only for the elderly. But now calligraphy has become a playground for many teenagers.

Calligraphy letters are valuable works of art and become very precious gifts. People ask for letters, give calligraphy letters to each other on many occasions throughout the year, not just encapsulated on New Year's Day as in the past. Many calligraphy clubs have been established, and many exhibitions of calligraphy works have been opened to help this traditional art penetrate into real life.

Main Typefaces

  • Chan Tu, also known as Chan Phuong. This is the easiest way to read and write, the handwriting is similar to lowercase letters.
  • The word Transformation is also known as Stylized word. Quite similar to the word Chan Tu, but modified to show the writer's own style.
  • Simulation letters are written based on the typefaces of other countries. Looking at Vietnamese calligraphy this style is similar to Japanese and Chinese characters...
  • The word Cuong Thao is also known as the word Private. This is the typeface that most clearly shows the style of the writer. This pen style is very liberal, free and flying. It is written seamlessly so it is quite difficult to read.
  • Woodblock script, this is the typeface usually engraved on wood. At a glance, this style looks like Chinese - Nom characters, but if you look closely, it's Vietnamese written backwards.

Writing style

  • Apricot style: The handwriting is thinly written in each soft stroke. This style is suitable for writing Latin script characters.
  • Bamboo style: This is a strong, attractive writing style. The lines are written in large, firm lines, bearing the spirit of Chinese calligraphy.


Basic Strokes

  • Cylindrical stroke: is a strong and fierce straight line.
  • Tonnage stroke: is a horizontal pulling stroke that is also full of power and strength.
  • Outline: including horizontal, vertical, oblique strokes... When writing, the pen starts and moves, but there is no stopping stroke, but it is dragged away.
  • Circle stroke: are the starting strokes and are pulled back into the last stroke towards the starting stroke.
  • Hook stroke: is the stroke that is started by hooking the pen down, but the end of the stroke is not towards the starting line, and when the pen is retracted, it does not stop but pulls the pen up to create a sharp line at the end of the stroke.
  • Drag strokes: are thin strokes extending in the horizontal, vertical or circular direction.
  • Curved lines: are curved strokes combined with wavy lines.
